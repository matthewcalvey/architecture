06 — How the Form Does the Work

Calvey Form: Sun Strategy

Cold Climate — Lean Back

The building form opens toward the sky. Low-angle winter sun enters through the facade at a near-perpendicular angle, penetrating deep into the interior. The roof mass, angled forward by the lean, naturally blocks high-angle summer sun. Stockholm, 59°N: winter noon sun at 7.3°, lean back 22°.

Mixed Climate — Moderate Lean

Eight heating months, four cooling. The form leans back to receive moderate-angle winter sun. When summer arrives, the high sun strikes the backward-leaning roof mass instead of entering the facade. New York, 41°N: winter 25.7°, summer 72.4°, lean back 10°.

Hot Climate — Lean Forward (Aggressive)

The form tilts toward the near-vertical sun. At equatorial latitudes where the sun passes directly overhead, the form leans forward aggressively — up to 23.4° — to deflect radiation and maximize the roof’s shading canopy. Equator, 0°: lean forward 23.4°.

Tropical Lean Progression: 0° – 20°

At low latitudes where cooling dominates, the forward lean is strongest. As latitude increases, forward lean relaxes until heating begins to matter.

01 — Origin

The Sun Is the Source of All Energy

The sun heats the atmosphere, drives the weather, grows the food, and warms the spaces we inhabit. Every climate on Earth is a function of how much solar energy a location receives and when it receives it.

Humans have always understood this. Pueblo cliff dwellings tucked under south-facing rock overhangs to capture low winter sun while staying cool in shade during summer.1 Persian wind towers channeled desert breezes while thick mud walls stored the night’s coolness.2 Greek and Roman cities were planned on solar grids — Socrates described the optimized house as one open to the winter sun and closed to the summer.3

The form of the building was inseparable from the climate it stood in.

02 — Divergence

Technology Broke the Connection

Steel, glass, and mechanical systems made it possible to build any shape, anywhere, and condition it artificially. A glass tower in Dubai runs the same HVAC logic as one in Helsinki. The curtain wall freed architecture from climatic logic.4

The energy cost of ignoring climate in form is enormous. Buildings account for a major share of global energy consumption, with a significant portion spent on heating and cooling that better form can reduce.5

03 — The Calvey Concept

Shape Buildings to Environments Like Plants Grow in Theirs

The architecture concept is to use passive systems that learn from indigenous practices for the environment and adapt them at various scales with organic building shapes that let sun in when you need heat and avoid it when you don't want heat gain — by the shape. Also shaped to channel energy, like wind, to activate passive ventilation and active energy generation systems. Also shaped to provide light to ground.

The mass and form is thanks to Gehry, and Bjarke Ingels; the passive systems are thanks to Frank Lloyd Wright and all indigenous strategies by the people, before mechanical systems.

Matthew Calvey, 2008 — Original Concept Notes

This study encodes sun geometry and climate demand into a standard chain that outputs an optimized lean angle from location alone.

Based on this concept there has to be an "optimized" form for a lean that lets the optimized ratio of sunlight in, per the climate. Like a tree without surrounding shade. The program square footage plus optimized room shapes and proportions by use for passive light and ventilation equals number of floors — which adapts to the sunlight gain angles and heat requirement per latitude and longitude climate data. This is what makes unique shapes: simply the sun angles and when you want it or not.

Matthew Calvey, 2026 — Optimized Forms Research Notes

The smooth, continuous surfaces envisioned in the concept serve solar and aerodynamic purposes simultaneously — channeling wind flows while optimizing incidence for solar gain or rejection. This page isolates the solar component: for a given location, what is the optimized lean angle?

04 — Two Knowable Facts

What Latitude Tells Us

When the building wants heat from sunlight — predicted from latitude using a regression trained on global climate data.6 The model estimates annual heating degree days (HDD) and cooling degree days (CDD).7

Where the sun is at those times — computed deterministically from latitude via solar geometry.8

Combined: which months want solar heat, and how high is the sun during those months. The optimal lean follows.

05 — The Formula Chain

Latitude In, Form Out

Step 1 — Predict Climate

A multivariate polynomial regression predicts heating and cooling demand from geographic coordinates:

HDD = 127.09 − 36.76·|lat| + 1.86·|lat|² + 0.43·elev − 2.41·coast + 81.61·(|lat|×coast/1000)
CDD = 2523.41 − 64.07·|lat| + 0.32·|lat|² − 0.92·elev + 0.58·coast − 1.41·(|lat|×coast/1000)

Heating fraction (HF = HDD / (HDD+CDD)) determines what portion of the year benefits from solar gain.

Step 2 — Compute Sun Angles

Solar noon altitude follows standard solar position equations:

δ = 23.45° × sin(2π × (284 + n) / 365)
α = 90° − |φ − δ|
