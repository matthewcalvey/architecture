# RFQ 006-26 vs. Website Review
## Port Tampa Bay Cruise Terminal 5 — Design Workshop Briefing

**Reviewed:** March 30, 2026
**Scope:** Cross-reference of website claims against publicly available RFQ context, news reporting, and internal data consistency.

> **Note:** The actual 47-page RFQ 006-26 PDF is not included in this repository and is not publicly indexed online. This review is based on what the website itself states the RFQ requires, cross-referenced against public reporting on Port Tampa Bay's fourth cruise terminal plans and verifiable third-party sources.

---

## Summary

The website is a high-quality design workshop briefing that demonstrates strong research, creative dual-use programming, and compelling site analysis. However, the review identified **several factual inaccuracies, one data inconsistency, and notable gaps** relative to what an RFQ response for this project should address.

**Verified accurate:** 10 major claims
**Inaccurate or misleading:** 3 claims
**Internal inconsistency:** 1 item
**Significant gaps:** 4 topics

## 2. Inaccuracies & Misleading Claims

### 2.1 "$1.2 billion Master Plan Vision 2030" — MISLEADING
- **Website says (line 304):** "Port Tampa Bay's Channelside district, within the $1.2 billion Master Plan Vision 2030 redevelopment envelope."
- **Reality:** The $1.2 billion figure refers specifically to the **channel deepening project** (deepening by 4 feet, in collaboration with the US Army Corps of Engineers), NOT the Master Plan Vision 2030 itself. The original Vision 2030 (released December 2016) was a strategic roadmap with $148.5M in investments planned 2017-2021. More recent reports (December 2025) cite an even higher $1.5 billion estimate for channel deepening.
- **Impact:** Attributing $1.2B to the "Master Plan Vision 2030" significantly overstates the redevelopment budget and could mislead reviewers about the scale of investment in the Channelside district specifically.
- **Fix:** Clarify that Vision 2030 is the strategic plan, and the $1.2B figure is for channel deepening specifically, or cite the broader capital investment figure accurately.

### 2.2 Ybor Harbor "5,000 residential units" — INFLATED
- **Website says (line 339):** "Ybor Harbor - 33 acres, rezoned April 2024: ~5,000 residential units, 500,000 SF office, 800 hotel rooms"
- **Reality:** The approved plan includes **4,750 residential units** (with 10% affordable housing), not 5,000. The office (500,000 SF) and hotel (800 rooms) figures are accurate.
- **Impact:** Minor inflation (~5%), but in a document being presented to a port authority, precision matters.
- **Fix:** Change "~5,000" to "4,750" or "~4,750."

### 2.3 Section Title Mismatch: "Five Critical Moves" vs. Three
- **Website (line 672):** `<h2>Five Critical Moves</h2>`
- **But section 05.0 (line 675):** "Three Critical Design Moves" listing only 3 moves (The Section, The Clear-Span Upper Hall, Site Organization).
- **Impact:** The h2 says "five" but the core moves section says "three." The five subsections (A-E) that follow could be interpreted as the "five," but this is confusing because they're labeled as separate "Design Opportunities & Ideas."
- **Fix:** Either change the h2 to "Three Critical Moves + Five Design Ideas" or restructure to be consistent.

## 4. Significant Gaps

### 4.1 Sunshine Skyway Bridge Height Restriction — NOT MENTIONED
- The **180-foot air draft restriction** imposed by the Sunshine Skyway Bridge is the single most consequential constraint on Port Tampa Bay's cruise market. It limits ships to roughly 2,000-2,500 passengers (current fleet air drafts).
- The website states a design day of 3,000 passengers and claims "RFQ requires planning for 4,500-5,000." Ships carrying 4,500-5,000 passengers would be significantly larger than what can currently pass under the Skyway Bridge.
- The website discusses the SSA Marine competing port (which exists precisely because of this constraint) but never explains *why* it's a threat or addresses the bridge limitation directly.
- **Impact:** An RFQ reviewer familiar with Tampa's port operations will notice this omission immediately. The growth scenario (4,500-5,000 pax) needs to be contextualized: is it predicated on channel deepening? Smaller ships doing double calls? Or a future Skyway bypass?
- **Recommendation:** Add a section explaining the Skyway constraint, how current capacity works within it, and what assumptions underpin the 4,500-5,000 growth scenario.

### 4.2 CBP/FIS Convertibility Claim — QUESTIONABLE
- Space B.02 (CBP/FIS, 6,000 SF) is marked as **"Full Convert"** to "indoor recreation: pickleball courts, volleyball, fitness events."
- Federal inspection spaces are governed by CBP's Cruise Terminal Design Standards. While some elements *can* be made demountable, the classification of the entire FIS zone as "Full Convert" is aggressive. CBP typically requires:
  - Secure, locked storage for inspection equipment
  - Interview rooms and secondary processing areas that maintain security features
  - APHIS inspection areas with specialized drainage
  - CCTV and access control that remain active
- The website correctly marks E.02 (CBP Office Suite) as "Fixed," but the primary FIS hall conversion claim should be softened to "Partial" or accompanied by a note about required CBP coordination.
- **Recommendation:** Reclassify B.02 as "Partial" or add explicit CBP coordination language.

### 4.3 No Budget or Cost Framework
- Public reporting indicates an **$80 million estimated investment** for the fourth terminal.
- The website provides no budget figure, cost-per-SF benchmarks, or cost framework.
- Comparable terminals cited on the website: Port Canaveral CT3 ($163M/188K SF = $867/SF), PortMiami Terminal A ($250M/170K SF = $1,471/SF).
- At 96,100 SF enclosed + 240,000 SF garage, even a conservative estimate would significantly exceed $80M.
- **Impact:** If the RFQ asks for preliminary cost awareness or budget alignment, this is a gap. The comparables section actually highlights how expensive similar terminals are without addressing what that means for T-5.
- **Recommendation:** Add a cost context section, even if high-level, referencing the comparable terminal cost-per-SF data already on the site.

### 4.4 HDR Engineering Already Under Contract
- In June 2024, Port Tampa Bay's board **unanimously approved a $500,000 contract with HDR Engineering** for engineering and design services on the fourth cruise terminal. Three other firms (Moffatt & Nichol, Burns & McDonnell, Kimley-Horn) also submitted proposals.
- The website does not acknowledge HDR's existing role or clarify how this design workshop relates to the procurement timeline.
- **Impact:** If this is an RFQ response for design services, an evaluator would want to understand the relationship to HDR's engineering work. If this is an independent/speculative exercise, that should be stated.
- **Recommendation:** Clarify the positioning — is this a complementary design vision to HDR's engineering, a competing pursuit, or an independent research exercise?

## 6. Strengths

The website does several things exceptionally well:

1. **Dual-use thesis is compelling and well-argued.** The 285-305 non-cruise days revenue opportunity is the strongest differentiator.
2. **Program development is thorough.** 36+ spaces with cruise/non-cruise convertibility ratings is rigorous.
3. **Contextual research is deep.** Ybor Harbor, THEA timeline, SSA Marine threat, Tampa WOW! — all verified accurate.
4. **Passenger flow logic is sound.** Two non-crossing loops (embark upper, debark grade) is an established best practice.
5. **Environmental strategy is appropriate.** ASHRAE 2A response, PV canopy, shore power, and FEMA flood zone awareness are all relevant.
6. **Transparency about AI collaboration** is a differentiator — documenting the process adds credibility rather than undermining it.
